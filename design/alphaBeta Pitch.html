The cost of running experiments is collapsing.
LLMs instrument tests, write analysis code, monitor dashboards, and synthesize findings. What used to take a team and a quarter now takes a person and a week.
The bottleneck is moving from "can we run this?" to "should we?"
Execution got cheap.
Judgment didn't.
When marginal cost drops to near-zero, the constraint isn't bandwidth. It's knowing which experiments deserve your conviction — and holding yourself to what you committed before the data came in.

Alpha — the risk of regret
The probability of a false positive — concluding something works when it doesn't. In practice: the cost of shipping a bad decision. The thing pre-registration is designed to control.
β
Beta — effort vs. certainty
The probability of a false negative — missing a real effect because you didn't invest enough to see it. The tradeoff between how hard you look and how sure you can be. The feasibility surface.

Hypotheses are academic. Bets are pragmatic.
The hypothesis
"If we move the plan-picker above the fold, then checkout-start will increase, because users currently bounce before seeing plans."
Structured. Testable in theory. But: no magnitude, no fold condition, no committed action. It describes what you'll measure — not what you'll do about it.
The bet
"I bet that moving the plan-picker above the fold would lift checkout-start by about 8%. I'm on a hunch, because replays show scroll drop-off at the testimonials. I'd fold if it comes in under +4%."
A magnitude. A confidence level admitted honestly. A mechanism. And a fold condition — the number that would make you stop. A bet can lose. That's the whole point.

The deviation is logged, in your words, beside the timestamp you can't move. Not blocked — recorded. Accountability through visibility, not compliance gates.
The loop closes
Per-bet learning: you expected +8% on a hunch; it came in at +2.5%. Calibration feeds the next wager.
Aggregate learning: patterns across many bets over time feed the next cycle's strategy. A bet's loss doesn't demote you — it withholds the rollout and saves the org from a bad release.
