"Start a new bet" — one action, nothing else. No onboarding wall.
Trigger: an idea they want to test. Could arrive from a Slack convo, a team meeting, or a shower thought.

Step 2: the tool reads back if / then / because. Flags missing falsifier.
→ Sharpen
Step 3: the wager sentence. Editable tokens. Losable indicator.

Win / inconclusive / loss actions pre-registered.

emotional arcwhat they feel
Excitement. "I have an idea."
Optimism bias is highest here. Everyone's idea feels like a winner.
Confidence — at first.
Friction. Reflection pushes back. "You didn't say why."
Honesty. Admitting it's a hunch, not conviction.
The falsifier gap: "I don't want to name what would make me stop." This is where the product either earns trust or feels preachy.
Sobriety. The cost of running becomes visible.
Maybe doubt. "Is this worth the traffic / time?"
This is the "should I?" moment — the product's central value prop, felt.
Weight. Locking feels serious. It should.
Relief. "It's declared. I can stop second-guessing."
Pre-registration as anxiety relief — you've committed, now just run it.
Anticipation. Waiting is hard.
Temptation. The urge to peek / interpret early.
For A/B: peek discipline is the method-specific intervention here (unbuilt). For interviews: the temptation is to start synthesizing mid-session.
Nerves. "What if I was wrong?"
The moment of truth. The product needs to feel like a safe place to be wrong — not a tribunal.
Clarity — if the criteria hold. "I know what this means."
Discomfort — if the call and the criteria diverge.
The deviation moment is the emotional climax: the product asks you to say why you're not doing what you committed to. This is where bad-faith pressure lands.
Closure. The bet is done. The record exists.
Growth. "I'd bet differently next time."
The journal entry is the payoff — proof that the discipline produced something real.
practitionerknows A/B, bad-faith org
Arrives with a formed hypothesis. May skip the dump and go straight to the wager (express lane).
Writes a tight dump. Reflection confirms rather than teaches. The push-back on the falsifier is accepted — they know what it means.
Risk: feels like overhead if the reflection isn't telling them anything new. The register dial matters here — Ambient mode.
Knows they want an A/B test. The nudge mostly validates. Feasibility surface is familiar territory.
The value for this persona is the locked record, not the feasibility math. They already know the math.
Understands the lock deeply. May hesitate — they know what immutability means in a bad-faith org.
The practitioner's fear: "if I lock this and it loses, my PM will use it against me." The product needs to frame the lock as protection, not exposure.
Familiar with waiting. Resists peeking. Knows the stats.
Returns confidently. Reads the number directly.
This is where the product earns its keep. The locked record is their shield: "I committed to +4%. It came in at +2.5%. The criteria say hold."
The deviation log is for this persona — when the PM says "ship it anyway," the practitioner has a record that was sealed before the result.
Calibration is meaningful — they have enough reps to see their own bias.
adjacent expertdeep domain, shaky stats
Arrives with strong domain conviction but vague methodology. "I know this will work, I just need to prove it."
Writes a rich dump — lots of domain reasoning, few numbers. The reflection does heavy lifting: extracts the mechanism, levels the confidence, surfaces the missing fold-if.
The reflection is the most valuable moment for this persona — it translates their domain knowledge into a testable bet without making them feel stupid.
Doesn't know what instrument to pick. The nudge does real work — "you don't need an A/B test, this is an observational question." The scaffolded register explains the stats.
"MDE slider" means nothing to them. The feasibility surface needs to speak in consequences ("3 weeks of traffic"), not methodology ("n per arm at 80% power").
May not fully grasp the weight of the lock. The discipline hint (toggleable) explains: "this freezes your commitment — later you'll be measured against it, not your post-hoc interpretation."
The onboarding affordance earns its place here. Without it, the adjacent expert locks without understanding why it matters.
Less familiar with waiting. May want to check progress. The "off-platform" phase is less comfortable.
Returns uncertain. "Is +3% good?" The fold-if they set earlier now does real work — it answers the question for them.
This is the payoff of the pre-registration for this persona: they don't have to decide if the number is good. They already decided, before the number existed.
The bucket is the product: "INCONCLUSIVE — your +3% is under the +4% fold-if you set." No ambiguity. No stats to interpret.
"But +3% seems good?" The discipline layer's hardest moment for this persona — the fold-if they set might feel arbitrary now. The product needs to hold firm but explain kindly.
Learning is where this persona grows fastest. "I set +4% but +3% felt like a win — next time I'll think harder about the threshold."
this is how the product teaches: not through tutorials but through the consequences of your own commitments
value momentswhere trust is earned
Zero-friction entry. No account, no onboarding quiz. Just start.
The reflection separates theory from confidence. The falsifier insistence makes it a real bet.
"Should I?" has somewhere to land. The cost is visible.
The lock is relief: the decision is made, documented, out of your head.
Off-platform. The lock holds while you don't.
The fold-if answers "is this good?" before you have to decide.
The deviation log: accountability as a feature, not a punishment.
The journal entry: proof the discipline produced something real.
risk / painwhere trust is lost
Low risk — unless the empty state is confusing.
The reflection feels preachy. The falsifier insistence feels like a lecture. The tone dial has to be right.
Feasibility math alienates the adjacent expert. The nudge feels prescriptive. "You don't know my domain."
The lock feels scary (practitioner) or meaningless (adjacent expert). Both are failure modes.
Peek temptation. No tool intervention (yet). The longest unattended gap.
Re-entry friction. Will they remember to come back? How does the tool reach them?
The deviation moment: preachy vs. permissive. Too preachy → routed around. Too permissive → meaningless.
Low risk — unless the learning field feels like homework.
